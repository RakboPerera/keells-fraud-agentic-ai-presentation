\# Supplier Risk Fraud Scenarios



\## Objective



This section illustrates realistic scenarios where supplier interactions and procurement processes may expose the retailer to financial or operational risk.



Supplier relationships involve large volumes of transactions, negotiated pricing agreements, and regular product deliveries. While most supplier activity follows normal procurement processes, unusual patterns may indicate pricing discrepancies, operational errors, or potential fraud.



The following scenarios demonstrate how supplier-related risks may emerge across procurement operations.



---



\## Scenario 1: Invoice Pricing Discrepancies



Suppliers invoice retailers for delivered goods based on agreed contract pricing.



Example pattern:



\- Invoices contain prices higher than agreed contract terms.

\- Pricing discrepancies occur repeatedly for specific products or suppliers.

\- Invoice values gradually increase across multiple delivery cycles.



Operational risk signal:



Invoice prices exceeding negotiated contract prices across repeated transactions.



---



\## Scenario 2: Delivery Quantity Mismatches



Products delivered by suppliers should match quantities specified in purchase orders.



Example pattern:



\- Delivered quantities are lower than purchase order quantities.

\- Receiving records consistently show discrepancies between delivered and recorded stock.

\- Certain suppliers exhibit repeated short deliveries.



Operational risk signal:



Repeated discrepancies between purchase order quantities and recorded delivery quantities.



---



\## Scenario 3: Unusual Supplier Price Changes



Suppliers may adjust product prices periodically due to market changes or renegotiated contracts.



Example pattern:



\- Sudden price increases for specific products without corresponding contract updates.

\- Price changes concentrated within certain suppliers.

\- Price increases inconsistent with market trends or comparable suppliers.



Operational risk signal:



Supplier pricing changes that deviate significantly from historical patterns.



---



\## Scenario 4: Supplier Concentration Risk



Retailers typically source products from multiple suppliers to maintain competitive pricing and supply resilience.



Example pattern:



\- Purchasing activity becomes heavily concentrated with a small number of suppliers.

\- Certain procurement teams consistently favor specific suppliers.

\- Supplier concentration increases without operational justification.



Operational risk signal:



Unusual concentration of purchase orders directed toward specific suppliers.



---



\## Scenario 5: Repeated Procurement Irregularities



Irregular procurement behavior may emerge when multiple discrepancies occur across purchasing activity.



Example pattern:



\- Purchase orders consistently modified after supplier selection.

\- Procurement approvals occurring outside normal governance processes.

\- Repeated contract exceptions granted to specific suppliers.



Operational risk signal:



Procurement activity patterns that deviate from standard governance procedures.



---



\## Why Supplier Risks Are Difficult to Detect



Supplier-related discrepancies often occur across multiple operational systems including procurement platforms, contract management systems, delivery records, and invoice processing systems.



Because these processes span several departments and systems, identifying irregularities may require analyzing patterns across multiple data sources.



Continuous monitoring of procurement signals helps identify emerging supplier risks earlier.



---



\## Role of the Supplier Risk Agent



The Supplier Risk Agent continuously analyzes supplier interactions across procurement and supply chain systems.



The system evaluates signals such as:



\- supplier pricing patterns

\- purchase order behavior

\- invoice value discrepancies

\- delivery quantity mismatches

\- supplier concentration across categories



By analyzing these signals together, the system can identify emerging supplier risks and generate investigation insights before financial losses escalate.
